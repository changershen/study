How Zendesk Uses Zendesk: The Collection Process

by Matthew Latkiewicz on May 14, 2010

Here at Zendesk, our relationship to our software is two-fold. On the one hand, it is software that we build  we write code, maintain servers, market to potential customers. On the other hand, it is software that we use  we collect support requests, handle incident reports, participate in the forums. This post describes one of the ways Zendesk actually uses Zendesk — in this case, how we collect tickets — and offers a blueprint of our own practices which may at least partially speak to your own best practices.

It should be noted that we constantly tweak and update how we use Zendesk as we grow and learn more about our customer’s needs and questions. Part of why we like Zendesk is this flexibility to grow and shift as our business changes.

Top Level Workflow: A Four Step Process

We’ve written before about the importance of defining a support process. After all, you could have the greatest tools in the world, but a help desk is only as good as the processes you define for it. We go into more detail about that process in the previous article; suffice it to say here that the workflow we currently employ with our Zendesk follows simple set of four steps:

  • Collect
  • Organize and Alert
  • Address and Communicate
  • Follow Through

In this post, we’re going to detail the first of those steps.

Collect, or how we get tickets into Zendesk

The easier you make it for customers to be in touch, the better you’ll be able to respond to and understand their needs. Zendesk offers a variety of few ways to collect support requests  email, the Feedback Tab (formerly called dropbox) in support.zendesk.com , the web portal, and the API. For our own implementation, we collect support requests using:

  • Email (and anything that is pointed to our email address such as Twitter DMs)
  • The web form on our support site (also called the ‘web portal’)
  • Phone Calls
  • Feedback Tab (it’s that “Support” widget on support.zendesk.com)
  • Our API

Email has been the predominant method by which we receive tickets, though a lot of web form tickets come in as well.

Currently, since our support agents add phone calls to the system by creating a new ticket via the web form, phone calls fall into the web form category.

Email Configuration

We like the ease that email offers our customers – they don’t have to learn a new environment; and because their emails are directly routed to Zendesk (as opposed to some other email inbox), we are sure to keep track of the whole conversation until it’s solved.

In order to handle our customer’s different types of concerns, we provide separate email addresses specific to those concerns. For instance, we have the email address “support@zendesk.com” for current and trial customers when they have questions specifically related to their Zendesk account; while we have the email address “jobs@zendesk.com” for employment inquiries.

We want route those incoming emails to different groups within Zendesk based on which email address it was sent to.  To do so, we first forward these various addresses on our mail server to a corresponding one within Zendesk.  Zendesk automatically handles these multiple addresses.  (For more on handling multiple incoming email addresses, check out our [intlink id="2179" type="post"]Tip of the Week [/intlink] about it.)

support@zendesk.com => support@support.zendesk.com

jobs@zendesk.com => jobs@support.zendesk.com

Figure 1

We have set up a trigger within Zendesk to verify that checks to see which email address was used and we then route it to the appropriate Group (groups within Zendesk are collections of agents; useful for limiting noise – each group only sees the tickets assigned to them). For jobs, this is the “Job Application” group (see Figure 1).

In addition to jobs@zendesk.com, we also route info@ and contact@zendesk through the same method (mail forwarding and triggers).  Those that don’t get routed to a specific group land in our First Level Support Group who will review and either address the ticket or escalate it to another group where appropriate.

The Web Support Form

For End-Users

figure 2

We use an open help desk — meaning users do not have to register or login to submit a ticket — so our support form is accessible from our support homepage (users click the “Submit a Request” link in the navbar).  We keep our help desk open because we want prospective customers to be able to reach us as well as those who have accounts already.

The form that users fill out to submit a request is customizable — go to Manage » Ticket Fields — so you can add your own fields to gather more information from your customer. We use the default form at Zendesk (see Figure 2) — just Subject and Description — and do most of the categorizing either manually or with Triggers. We do this intentionally to make the form is as easy as possible to fill out for the customer.  We also allow users to upload attachments to their tickets.

For Support Agents

Figure 3

Our support agents can also create new tickets using the web form, though theirs is a more extensive version of what our end-users fill out (see figure 3).  As we said, above this form is customizable.  In addition to the Zendesk system fields (Status, Type, Priority, Group, and Assignee), our internal form also captures:

  • About – a general description of what the ticket is about: i.e. Billing Question, or Bug Report, or Trial Extension, etc.  These categories shift over time as patterns emerge from the tickets we receive.  This helps us report later on the kinds of tickets we are receiving.
  • Feature – if the ticket is asking about a particular feature within Zendesk (i.e. the dropbox, or triggers, or the API), we record it here.  This helps us pinpoint problems with a particular feature, or to highlight when we may need to add more/better documentation.
  • Plan – we record what Zendesk plan the customer is on (if any).

Phone Calls

We offer phone support to our Plus+ customers.  Technically, phone calls are an extension of the Web Form; when a call comes in (our number is listed on our contact page), the agent creates the ticket using the web form we described above (capturing any relevant customer data).  They record the question and how they responded if they are able to answer it.  If they aren’t able to answer the caller’s question, they escalate the ticket to another Group and the rest of the interaction is handled through email.

If the caller leaves a voicemail, our PBX system sends us an email with the voicemail attached. This email then routes into Zendesk and we treat it like a normal standard ticket.

Dropbox

The dropbox is a script that can put a support form on any of your webpages.  It creates a tab on the side of that webpage that when clicked launches the form.

We put the dropbox on each page of our website (except for a few of the signup pages). While a site visitor can always reach our support portal through a link at the top of the page, we want them to be able to send us a ticket without having to leave the site.  We do this for two reasons:

  1. If a customer or perspective customer is on our site and has a question or problem, we want to make their ability to reach us a seamless as possible.  Asking them to click a link or open up their email program puts a barrier between them and us that will discourage folks.  Discouraged folks are what we’re trying to avoid.
  2. Our site is where we sell our product.  We don’t really want visitors to leave the site if they don’t have to.

We added the Dropbox within the past six months, and while it isn’t that significant a percentage of our tickets yet, it is larger this year compared to all-time.

Web Service; or API

Periodically, we will sent emails out to folks whose Zendesk trials have lapsed with a special offer if they reactivate their trial.  To reactivate, they click a link from the email.  This creates a new ticket within Zendesk through our API.  While we won’t get into the technical details here, we can route that ticket using triggers, and get the trial reactivation to a specific support agent in charge of reactivated trials.

Start a Collection

No good support process can work without establishing an effective means of collecting requests from your users and/or customers.  It’s important that your customers can reach you with minimal frustration, and at the points where their questions and issues arise.   And when they reach out to you, it’s important that you are able to collect all their messages.

How do you collect your support tickets?  What works for you and your organization?  Let us know in the comments.
