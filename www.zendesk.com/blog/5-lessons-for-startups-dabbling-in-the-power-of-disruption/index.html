5 Lessons for Startups Dabbling in the Power of Disruption

by Maksim Ovsyannikov on October 1, 2010

With the conclusion of TechCrunch Disrupt San Francisco, I wanted to send a big thank you to the entire TechCrunch team for organizing a great event, and also organize some thoughts inspired by experiences I had on the conference floor.

The forum was intense, characterized by entrepreneurial appetites for greatness, passion, and an overall drive to innovate.

Turning ideas into meaningful, influential products requires a lot of momentum and some superpower (power = the rate at which work is performed and energy is converted). I’d like to share five personal and organizational powers that I’ve seen help businesses get off the ground, grow, and most importantly create deep relationships with customers. They have the added benefit of causing great disruption as well!

1. Showing up: The power of presence

“90 percent of life is just showing up.” -Woody Allen

That old quote rang true for me strolling Startup Alley at Disrupt. Just being at the event was a major step many young companies took in their development. Allens quote might be overused or maybe even cliché, but its also very true. Virtual collaboration, digital, and other remote communications are efficient and helpful, but face-to-face interactions are game-changing.

Many of the folks my colleagues and I spoke with literally launched their products at 1 a.m., the same day of the event! Still, investing time and money to attend the event was one of the most important steps these young companies could have taken to develop a better product and presence for the customers whom they already serve or are about to acquire.

2. Speaking up: The power of voice

It doesn’t matter who runs the meeting; the de facto leader is the one who frames the debate and articulates the consensus. Yes, Michael Arrington ran the show, but it was all of the CEOs, co-founders, investors, and entrepreneurs who carried the conversation at the event.

The power of the conversations at Disrupt came from the ability of all those people to put into words the themes that shape their action. My hat goes off to everyone who spoke from the stage; shared opinions, knowledge, and experience on the floor in Startup Alley; or simply brought an idea with an exclamation point to the event with the chutzpuh to share it.  We change the world by speaking up and advocating for our customers and for the tech community at large.

3. Teaming up: The power of partnering

I was amazed at the power of real-time face-to-face collaboration at the event. I was deeply inspired by college graduate, Jaime Ohm, who won the Hackathon. She was able to attract developers, coders, and other collaborators to her project; most importantly she ended her moment on stage with a slide that read:

  • I may turn this into a company.
  • I would love to collaborate with you.
  • I would love to meet you for coffee in the SF Bay area.
  • I am looking for a mentor.

It was a reminder to me that most things worth achieving can’t be done alone.

  • Entrepreneurs need co-founders.
  • Innovators in established companies need a coalition of backers and supporters.
  • Quarterbacks need linebackers.
  • Surgeons need interns, scrub nurses, and many hands on deck.
  • Even the president needs a cabinet!

Partnership invites reciprocity  giving as well as getting. It’s a true ecosystem!

4. Looking up: The power of values

Higher principles help people transcend immediate conflicts and concerns. Standing for something larger than mere self-interest gives leaders moral grounding and provides a basis for inspiring and motivating the work.

Values also attract customers. I couldn’t find a single company at Disrupt that wasnt able to articulate a set values they already had or were trying to build. Values are brand power that matters to customers. Customers give your ideas more value.

5. Not giving up: The power of persistence

Everything can look like a failure in the middle. Harvards Rosabeth Moss Kanter

A prevailing theme at Disrupt was Keep at it! — learn to make mid-course adjustments; keep pivoting, surprising, and impressing the naysayers.

Change agents in companies sometimes get approval after repeated turndowns just because they wear out their critics who run out of arguments. Questioned by panels of expert judges, competitors and critics from the media, there was a uniform amount of perseverance among the entrepreneurs.

If I could sum up the best “disruption” it wouldn’t be an observation about the power of a particular product, it would be the sheer power of people, innovators and customers.
