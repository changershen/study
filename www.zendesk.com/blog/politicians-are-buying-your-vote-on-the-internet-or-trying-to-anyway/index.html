Politicians Are Buying Your Vote on the Internet (Or Trying to Anyway)

by Lydia Neptune on November 1, 2010

In the interest of being the best darn educated voter you can be this election season, we know youve diligently combed the Internet in search of the best possible information on candidates, propositions, and all other things that will ultimately end up as a vote on a ballot. Nice job.

Were curious though. Did you notice by chance that whenever you Google the name of a candidate for whom you are considering voting, the page is littered with paid advertisements trying to entice you to click through to websites that discredit your candidate. These ads are purchased, of course, by the competition. Are you swayed? Did the candidate with the deeper pockets just buy your vote via Google?

Carly Fiorina vs. Barbara Boxer

In California, the well-financed supporters of senatorial candidate Carly Fiorina, combined with tech-savvy campaigners, have ensured that anyone searching for the term Barbara Boxer is greeted with a laundry list of anti-Boxer websites, including FailedSenator.com/BarbaraBoxer and CarlyforCalifornia.com. Several of the exact same sites also come up in sponsored search results underFiorina’s name as well, where, interestingly, no anti-Fiorina/pro-Boxer sites show up. The GOP has effectively outbid the Boxer campaign on both of the candidates names. Arizonas East Valley Tribunenotes that the tactic has also been used in the hotly-contested gubernatorial campaign there this year, with one expert calling the strategy a best practice. And in Great Britain, the Conservative party bought out the terms “Budget”, “Pre-Budget Report” and “Treasury” late last year to ensure that Googlers were led to anti-Labour party websites providing the Tories take on financial matters.

Is this any worse than paying for overwhelming dominance of television airtime or swamping voters with anti-opposition junk mail? It seems that the key difference is the notion of fooling a voter into searching for one candidates name and essentially receiving literature from a rival. Sure, the links are listed as sponsored, but even the limited space that campaigners have to work with under each link can pack a powerful subliminal punch  especially when the sheer number of sponsored ads gives a massive part of the screen to an opposing candidate.

When British Petroleum bought out the term oil spill earlier this year to try to lead searchers to a BP public-relations site, customers who were offended by the ploy took to the web to condemn the practice, including launching a fake Twitter account to mock BP. Hopefully, voters have been savvy enough to understand which search results are purchased versus those that are organic. And such tactics will also turn them, like the BP critics, off. Or is this simply another case of deep pockets ruling politics? The courts will decide the appropriateness of AdWord piggy-back strategies in the case of buying flowers, but so far theres no arbiter on the morality of the strategy for buying votes.

Businesses do it all the time

Companies have long bid on competitors names for search engine pay-per-click advertising. The wisdom of this strategy is debatable  some experts claim that the practice is ineffective, regardless of any moral or ethical considerations. However, plenty of businesses do it anyways in hopes of pulling customers away from their rivals.

In fact, a case is heading to European court in which floral giant FTDs international segment Interflora is suing U.K. retailer Marks & Spencer for trademark infringement for piggy-backing on Google AdWords searches. M&S bid on the trademarked Interflora name so that whenever a customer typed Interflora into the search box, a link to the Marks & Spencer floral website would pop up in the sponsored links section, directly beneath Interfloras own site. (The paid search results have since changed, presumably due to the pending suit.)

I just tried it out for myself as I was shopping for a website to print out some digital photos. Type online photo-printing site name shutterfly into the Google search box. The sponsored links window features competitor snapfish.com.

Now type snapfish into that same search box. What comes up second in the sponsored link sections is, of course, the shutterfly.com site.

Its a pretty standard practice among Internet advertisers, though one that could change depending on the precedent set by the Interflora suit.

You can see how while this could be troublesome to companies who have business stolen away by such tactics, it could at the same time benefit consumers. Who knows, maybe those roses are a better deal at Marks & Spencer? Might as well click and check it out.

But what are the implications when this sort of strategy is being used by politicians? In a country where butterfly ballots proved confusing enough to sway the course of a presidential election, could manipulation of Internet search results coerce unsuspecting voters?
